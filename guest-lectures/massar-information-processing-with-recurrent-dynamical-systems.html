Information processing with recurrent dynamical systems: theory, characterisation and experiment

Information processing with recurrent dynamical systems: theory, characterisation and experiment

Submitted by Nathan Labhart on Tue, 2011-11-01 17:08
Presenter: 
Serge Massar
Location: 
ICMC 2011, Venice
Country: 
Italy
Video Link: 
SWITCHcast
Slides: 
ICMC2011_Slide_Serge_Massar.pdf
Summary: 

In this talk I will present work our group at Universit Libre de Bruxelles has realized, in close collaboration with the Reservoir Lab of Gent University, on information processing with recurrent dynamical systems.
Recurrent dynamical systems are notoriously difficult to train and their use in the machine learning community only really took off with the advent of reservoir computing. The main idea of reservoir computing is that many dynamical systems are capable of high level information processing provided they operate in the appropriate dynamical regime. The difficulty of training recurrent dynamical systems is circumvented by only training a feedforward output layer.

Here we will report progress on three important questions related to reservoir computing: Can we develop a theory of information processing by recurrent dynamical systems? Can we develop general methods to characterize the information processing that takes place in such systems? Can we use the concept of reservoir computing for experimental realization of analog information processing systems?

Concerning the first topic, we will discuss how the mean field techniques from statistical mechanics enables one to describe the dynamics of large networks of randomly interconnected nodes driven by an external time dependent signal (i.e. echo state networks). We will show how predictions on the variance of the variables and on the distribution of their variances can be obtained. We will also make contact with the recent work of Hermans and Schrauwen who showed that infinite reservoirs can be viewed as recursive kernels.

The second topic deals with the characterization of the information processing that takes place within dynamical systems when they are driven by a time dependent input. The main tool developed so far (by Jaeger in 2002) is the linear memory function which characterizes how well the dynamical system can recall past inputs. In particular Jaeger showed that the integral of the linear memory function is bounded by the size of the network, and can equal the size of the network only in the case of linear networks. Here we extend this notion to the non linear memory capacity which characterizes how well the network can reconstruct both linear and non linear functions of the past inputs. Furthermore, we show that if one sums these capacities over an appropriate set of functions (they must constitute, in a specific sense, a complete orthonormal set), then the total capacity obtained is always smaller than the size of the network, with equality obtained if the network has fading memory. Note how these two results parallel the two results of Jaeger concerning the linear memory capacity. By using these new notions, it is possible to carry out a detailed and systematic study of the information processing capabilities of non linear networks driven by a time dependent input. We illustrate this notion by computing numerically the non linear memory capacities for echo state networks for a range of parameters.

Concerning the third topic, we have developed an opto-electronic architecture which allows very simple experimental realization of reservoir computers, as it consists of only a very small number of components. This architecture consists of a single non linear node and a delay line, and uses time multiplexing (rather than spatial multiplexing as is usual in echo state networks). In our experiment the non linear node is a fiber optic intensity modulator, and the delay line consists of a fiber spool. It operates at approximately 100kHz, and can implement networks of size up to 200 nodes. It is fully automated by interfacing with a computer. Using this system we obtain excellent performance on different benchmark tasks, such as speech recognition, signal classification, channel equalization. We also make contact with the previous topic by measuring its linear and non linear memory capacities.

Finally I will emphasize where, in my opinion, future challenges lie. For instance I will discuss whether we can develop a comprehensive theory of information processing by recurrent dynamical systems? How broadly can one apply the new notion of non linear memory capacity? What are the future directions for experimental implementation of reservoir computing?

Presenter Information: 

Serge Massar was born in Zambia in 1970 and passed most of his youth in Africa. He graduated in physics from the Universite ́ Libre de Bruxelles (ULB) in 1991 with highest honours. He then began a PhD in theoretical physics under the direction of Robert Brout, studying black hole evaporation and related questions dealing with particle creation in external fields. He defended his PhD in 1995 with highest honours. From 1995 to 1997 he was a post-doctoral researcher at Tel Aviv University (Israel) in the group of Y. Aharonov, and then from 1997 to 1998 at Utrecht University (Netherlands) in the group of G. ’t Hooft. During this period he got increasingly involved in the nascent field of quantum information. In 1998 he came back to the ULB as a Research Associate of the Fonds National de la Recherche Scientifique (FNRS), and since 2008 he is Research Director of the FNRS. Since 2004 he directs the Laboratoire d’Information Quantique at ULB. His main research topics nowadays are quantum information sciences, both from the point of view of theory and from the point of view of implementations in quantum optics. He also has a new interest in machine learning and information processing with dynamical systems. He has co-authored more than 90 publications in leading physics journals, totalling more than 2500 citations. In 2003 he was awarded the Alcatel-Bell prize of the FNRS for his research on experimental quantum information processing, and in 2010 the ”Prix du Ministere” of the french journal La Recherche for his work on non locality and quantum random number generators.
